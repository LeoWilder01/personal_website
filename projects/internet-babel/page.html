Background

[In an era marked by increasing fragmentation, cultural divergence, and the breakdown of shared public discourse, this personal context has become both more influential and more opaque. Disagreements rarely stem from isolated opinions alone; more often, they emerge from deeply accumulated experiences, cultural trajectories, and unspoken assumptions that never fully surface in language. As a result, communication increasingly fails not because people lack words, but because they lack access to one another's contexts. ]

What it does

[The Method this broadcast trying to propose is a representation learning-oriented project focused on social interaction, semantic understanding, and cultural immersion. It can be understood as an “Internet Reverse Tower of Babel.” It is based on a very simple idea: context as a language. Rather than understanding one another only through the final outputs of what they actually said, let’s now aims to understand each other through the process of thinking itself, through context and background information. Whether at the level of ethnic groups, cultures, or communities, all such entities evolve internally over long periods of time. Language can be translated, but meaning often does not circulate. For example, memes or in-group jokes that emerge within a culture are frequently difficult to truly understand once they cross into another culture, even after translation, The same applies at the individual level. Every person undergoes a long, unique, and complex process of development. These experiences support cognition and every decision a person makes, while also creating distance between people. Starting from cultural phenomena or semantic outcomes that are already formed yet difficult for outsiders to understand, using agents, we traces their evolution across the internet, analyzes how meaning shifts at key moments, and examines how collective usage gradually reinforces or distorts original semantics. Over time, these evolutionary chains together construct a “Tower of Babel,” and by moving downward along these chains, people can gradually access the full context of others. ]
